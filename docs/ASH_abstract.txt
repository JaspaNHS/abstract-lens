Introduction: Conference abstracts are a cornerstone of the grey literature. For many investigators they are the simplest and fastest way to disseminate results, and frequently the only public record of independent research that has not been validated by industry. Yet the annual output is vast: the 2025 American Society of Hematology (ASH) meeting alone comprises 8,255 abstracts, and this corpus is poorly served by conventional search engines. This makes it hard for investigators to judge whether an idea is novel or has recently been explored, and for reviewers to place a submission in context. We built Abstract Lens, a proof-of-concept interactive assistant that answers questions using only a defined, meeting-specific corpus, and we assessed its reliability.

Methods: All 8,255 abstracts of the 67th ASH Annual Meeting (Blood 2025;146[Suppl 1]) were retrieved through the publisher's official application programming interface under its text-and-data-mining licence. Text was extracted; records whose file lacked a body were recovered from the full-text service (final records without a body, 0). Each abstract was embedded and stored in a vector database, and every record was tagged with its presentation tier (plenary, oral, poster, or online-only). For each question the system retrieves the closest-matching abstracts, re-ranks them by tier, and passes them to a large language model constrained to (i) use only the retrieved text, (ii) attach an in-text citation to every factual statement, and (iii) explicitly decline when the corpus does not contain the answer. No external knowledge is permitted, and verbatim source text shown to users is capped for licence compliance. Reliability was tested with adversarial questions (fabricated trials or drugs absent from the meeting, false-premise questions, and deliberately ambiguous questions) and by an independent automated judge scoring the proportion of cited statements actually supported by the cited abstract. The assistant was further reviewed by independent reviewers from 3 centres external to the principal investigator's institution, who posed domain-specific questions in hematopoietic-cell transplantation, erythropathology, and general oncohematology.

Results: Ingestion was complete and lossless (8,255/8,255). Under adversarial testing the assistant did not fabricate: it declined every question about trials or drugs not present in the corpus, rejected false premises rather than accepting them, and requested clarification for ambiguous queries, while answering in-scope questions with citations traceable to individual abstracts. Citation faithfulness on exploratory queries was 92 to 100% by the independent judge. Reviewers from the 3 external centres confirmed that answers were grounded in and traceable to the cited abstracts, that higher-tier evidence was prioritised, and that out-of-scope questions were appropriately declined across transplantation, erythropathology, and general oncohematology. A working instance and the full source code are openly available as evidence of function (application: https://var-five-sharing-parking.trycloudflare.com; code: https://github.com/JaspaNHS/abstract-lens).

Conclusions: Abstract Lens shows that a citation-grounded, corpus-restricted conversational assistant can be built over the grey literature of a single meeting and behaves reliably under both adversarial and expert questioning. Such a tool can help investigators assess the originality of an idea and detect recent, hard-to-find evaluations beyond standard search engines, and can help reviewers contextualise submissions. The methodology is directly extensible: feeding the same architecture with therapeutic guidelines from national or international organisations could yield trustworthy, easily consulted and distributable references. Formal accuracy validation against an expert-curated reference set is ongoing.
